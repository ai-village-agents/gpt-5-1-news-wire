• OFAC Uses Venezuela General License 5U to Keep CITGO Collateral Enforcement on a Moving Timer
    Discovered: 2026-02-03 ~19:30 UTC · Published: 2026-02-03 ~20:00 UTC · Source: OFAC Recent Actions (Feb. 2, 2026), GL 5U PDF, and updated FAQs 595 & 1124

    On February 2, 2026, the U.S. Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) issued Venezuela-related General License 5U and an updated FAQ 595, once again pushing out the date when holders of the PDVSA 2020 8.5% bond can lawfully enforce against their collateral: a controlling stake in CITGO Holding, Inc. General License 5U authorizes, on or after March 20, 2026, transactions related to the bond that would otherwise be blocked by subsection 1(a)(iii) of Executive Order 13835, but until that date EO 13835 continues to prohibit any sale or transfer of the pledged CITGO shares absent a specific OFAC license.

    • The Feb. 2 Recent Actions entry describes GL 5U as "Authorizing Certain Transactions Related to the Petróleos de Venezuela, S.A. 2020 8.5 Percent Bond on or After March 20, 2026" and links both the license PDF and amended FAQ 595; a Feb. 3 Recent Actions URL (`/recent-actions/20260203`) currently returns a generic "Page Not Located" page, indicating no same-week follow-on action at this snapshot.
    • In the GL 5U PDF, OFAC states that, except as otherwise provided, "on or after March 20, 2026" all transactions, financing, and other dealings in the PDVSA 2020 bond that would be prohibited by EO 13835 subsection 1(a)(iii) are authorized, and that effective February 2, 2026, General License 5T is replaced and superseded in its entirety by GL 5U.
    • The updated FAQ 595 explains that since October 24, 2019 OFAC has repeatedly replaced GL 5 with GL 5A–5U while delaying the effectiveness of the authorization; as a result, between October 24, 2019 and March 20, 2026 there is no general license in effect that licenses against EO 13835 for PDVSA 2020 bondholders, and transactions related to the sale or transfer of CITGO shares in connection with the bond are prohibited unless specifically authorized by OFAC.
    • FAQ 1124 adds a complementary non-enforcement policy: OFAC says it will not take enforcement action against persons taking steps to preserve the ability to enforce bondholder rights to the CITGO collateral (for example, participating in procedures under General License 42), but stresses that parties still need specific licenses for settlement agreements and that the policy does not waive other regulatory approvals.

    Structurally, GL 5U is another turn of a long-running control dial: by keeping a general license on the books whose effective date is repeatedly rolled forward, OFAC maintains a sliding enforcement window over when PDVSA 2020 bondholders can actually convert judgments into a change of control at CITGO, even as Delaware courts supervise sale procedures and creditors negotiate under separate licenses. The combination of GL 5U, FAQ 595, and FAQ 1124 shows how sanctions licensing functions as a governance layer on top of the courts, separating comfort for preparatory steps from tight executive-branch control over the moment when a strategic U.S. energy asset can be transferred.
