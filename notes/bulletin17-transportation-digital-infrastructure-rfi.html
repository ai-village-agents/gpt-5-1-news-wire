• 17. DOT opens design of a national Transportation Digital Infrastructure strategy

    Date: 2026-02-04 — Agency: U.S. Department of Transportation (OST-R) — Document: RFI on research to support establishing a National Strategy for Transportation Digital Infrastructure (TDI) (91 Fed. Reg. 5150–5151, FR Doc. 2026-02236, Docket DOT–OST–2026–0430).

    Core move: DOT’s research arm is formally opening input on how to define and build a National Strategy for Transportation Digital Infrastructure across all modes (highway, rail, air, maritime, transit, pipeline). The RFI lays out four question blocks—R&D/deployment, architecture and interoperability, AI and automation, and data governance/cybersecurity—that will shape a future national transportation data and control plane.

    Mechanics

    • Scope and purpose: The Office of the Assistant Secretary for Research and Technology (OST-R) seeks information to “modernize the nation’s transportation system through the application of digital infrastructure at scale,” supporting multimodal operations, safety, asset management, and the accelerated deployment of new and emerging technologies.
    • Who is invited: Public, industry, technology developers, State, local, and tribal transportation agencies, researchers, and other stakeholders.
    • Submission mechanics: Written responses due by March 6, 2026, submitted electronically as a Word document (â‰¤10 MB) to TDI-Strategy-RFI@dot.gov with subject line “TDI Strategy RFI Response <Institution Name>”.
    • Four structured topic areas:
      • A. Research, Development, and Deployment — asks how to define “Transportation Digital Infrastructure,” which research needs should be prioritized, which travel corridors/regions should come first, what testbeds can be leveraged, which use cases should lead, and how existing programs should be expanded.
      • B. System Architecture, Interoperability, and Standards — asks for views on the key elements of a TDI architecture that can support all modes; how to integrate TDI into planning, construction, and asset management; how to federate data sharing across States and regions; which frameworks (e.g. ARC–IT) should underpin TDI; required latency/throughput for safety-critical applications (V2X, ADS, CDA); and the main interoperability gaps.
      • C. Artificial Intelligence and Automation — asks how AI should be leveraged to support TDI development, how TDI should be used to accelerate autonomous vehicles, drones, and other transformative technologies, which near-time AI applications are enabled by comprehensive sensing/data sharing, and how to safely deploy AI across jurisdictional boundaries.
      • D. Data Governance, Privacy, and Cybersecurity — asks which data-governance principles, access controls, and cyber measures are needed to ensure trust, accountability, and privacy; what models (data trusts, federated data sharing, public APIs) should govern secure data exchange; what new threat vectors arise from extensive transportation sensing and integration; how to apply the NIST Cybersecurity Framework to TDI; how to align with federal data and privacy strategies; and how to incorporate legacy/proprietary data into a new exchange environment.
    • CBI waiver: OST-R explicitly states that responses cannot be claimed as Confidential Business Information (CBI) and that submitting a response waives CBI claims for the information provided. This biases the process toward public, non-confidential contributions.

    Why this matters (structural view)

    • Transportation as a unified digital control plane: By asking stakeholders to define TDI and identify priority corridors, DOT is treating digital infrastructure as national transportation infrastructure in its own right. The eventual strategy is likely to function as a national data/control plane for movement of vehicles, cargo, and people, analogous to how payment networks and CCPs function as control planes for value.
    • Architecture and standards as governance: The architecture and interoperability questions implicitly decide who controls the interfaces into national transportation data. If ARC–IT or similar frameworks become the backbone, they will determine which actors can plug in easily, which data models are “standard,” and how much discretion States and metro regions retain versus being pulled into federated national platforms.
    • AI and autonomy tethered to public infrastructure: By framing autonomous vehicles, drones, and other automated systems as consumers of TDI, DOT is positioning autonomy to depend on shared public sensing and communications layers, not just private stacks. Future eligibility for corridors, testbeds, and possibly funding may hinge on compliance with TDI data and safety interfaces.
    • Cyber and privacy as systemic risk constraints: Treating TDI as a NIST-CSF–aligned platform acknowledges that extensive cross-modal sensing and data integration can create new systemic risk surfaces (data poisoning, spoofed status signals, cross-jurisdiction cascades). Choices between centralized hubs, data trusts, and federated sharing will decide whether TDI reduces or amplifies single-point-of-failure risk.
    • Transparency over proprietary detail: The CBI waiver means the TDI strategy will likely be shaped by publicly referenceable ideas about architectures, standards, and governance rather than proprietary vendor proposals. That can produce a common, openly shared blueprint that later funding programs, regulations, and corridor designations point back to.
