• 21. FCC activates Robocall Mitigation Database as an annual recertification gatekeeper

    Date: 2026-02-05 — Regulator: Federal Communications Commission (FCC) — Document: Federal Register notice FR Doc. 2026-02311, 91 FR 5242–5243, Improving the Effectiveness of the Robocall Mitigation Database; CORES Registration System (Paperwork Reduction Act effective-date notice implementing FCC 24-135, 91 FR 343).

    Core move: The FCC is not rewriting the robocall rules here; instead, this Paperwork Reduction Act (PRA) notice flips key obligations in FCC 24-135 from paper to live. By announcing OMB approval for the new information collections in 47 CFR parts 1 and 64 (including §§ 1.8002(b)(2) and 64.6305(h)), the Commission activates an annual recertification regime for the Robocall Mitigation Database (RMD) that is tightly bound to the FCC's CORES/FRN identity system. Voice service providers and intermediate providers must now log into CORES, confirm and update their RMD filings, and recertify every year rather than treating RMD as a one-time registration.

    What this PRA notice actually does

    • Sets the PRA effective date: Confirms that OMB has approved the relevant information collections under control numbers 3060-0918 and 3060-1285, and makes the associated RMD/CORES requirements effective as of 2026-02-05.
    • Starts the recertification clock: Ties the first annual RMD recertification deadline to 2026-03-01. Providers that previously registered must log in and recertify by that date, and then annually, or risk being treated as out of compliance.
    • Binds RMD to CORES/FRN identity: Providers can no longer treat RMD as a standalone portal. The PRA notice confirms that CORES FRN credentials are the gatekeeper for accessing and updating RMD entries, turning FRNs into the canonical identity key for who is allowed to originate calls on U.S. networks.
    • Clarifies enforcement leverage: Under the underlying order, providers that fail to register or keep their RMD filings current can have their traffic blocked by other carriers. With PRA approval in place, those blocking obligations and related certification duties now sit on a firmer legal foundation.

    Why this is structural

    • RMD as a call-origination control plane: The RMD already functions as a routing whitelist: if a voice service provider or intermediate provider is missing or non-compliant, others are expected to block their traffic. Moving from one-time registration to mandatory annual recertification turns the RMD into a living authorization and identity control plane for who is allowed to originate calls into the U.S. telephone network.
    • PRA timing defines the real enforcement calendar: The substantive robocall rules in FCC 24-135 technically took effect earlier, but without OMB approval the associated data collections could not be fully enforced. This notice quietly sets the true start date for many of the practical compliance obligations and makes March 1, 2026 a hard timing hook for providers and their counsel.
    • CORES as a systemic identity anchor: By requiring providers to use CORES and maintain a valid FRN to manage RMD entries, the FCC is further consolidating provider identity, contact information, and enforcement history around a single credentialing system. That strengthens the Commission's ability to correlate filings across proceedings and to tie robocall mitigation behavior back to specific legal entities.
    • Network-level consequences for paperwork failures: Because RMD status flows into network-wide blocking obligations, a missed recertification or stale filing is not just a paperwork violation. It can translate directly into call routing changes and loss of service reachability, giving this PRA notice outsized structural impact compared with a typical back-office form approval.
