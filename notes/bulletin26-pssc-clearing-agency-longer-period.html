• 26. SEC slows decision on a DLT-based Paxos clearing agency that would plug directly into DTC.

    On February 3, 2026, the Securities and Exchange Commission published a one-page notice designating a 90-day extension of the deadline to decide whether Paxos Securities Settlement Company, LLC (PSSC) can register as a clearing agency under Exchange Act section 17A. The notice points to PSSC's "novel" private, permissioned distributed-ledger settlement service—designed for bilateral delivery-versus-payment (DvP) settlement and intended to operate as a participant in the Depository Trust Company (DTC)—and states that the Commission needs more time to assess whether the application satisfies section 17A's risk-management and default-management standards.

    What the notice does

    • Freezes the clock at the edge of the clearing-agency perimeter. Under Exchange Act section 19(a)(1)(B) (15 U.S.C. 78s(a)(1)(B)), once an application for clearing-agency registration is noticed in the Federal Register, the SEC normally has 180 days to conclude proceedings by order granting or denying registration. The statute allows a single extension of up to 90 days for good cause.
    • Formally extends PSSC's deadline. The initial notice of PSSC's Form CA-1 application (Release No. 34-103624, 90 FR 37940) was published on August 6, 2025, starting the 180-day clock. The Frontier date for a decision was therefore February 2, 2026. The new notice extends that period by 90 days and designates May 3, 2026 as the date by which the Commission must either grant or deny the application.
    • Ties the extension to a very specific technology and topology. The Commission states that PSSC would provide services via a private, permissioned settlement service supporting a distributed ledger, designed to conduct bilateral DvP settlement, and that PSSC would become a DTC participant so that its services can be made available to other DTC participants. The notice characterizes this as a "novel structure" that affects PSSC's risk management, risk surveillance, and rules for managing participant defaults, and cites these features as the reason for invoking the 90-day extension.
    • Anchors the extension in the prior proceedings. The notice recaps that the SEC has already instituted proceedings (the November 4, 2025 OIP, Release No. 34-104174, 90 FR 51416) to determine whether PSSC's application is consistent with section 17A(b)(3), and that at least one public comment was received on the application.

    How this rewires (or delays rewiring) the settlement control plane

    • A potential new clearing node inside the DTC fabric. PSSC is not proposing a peripheral fintech service; it is seeking full clearing-agency registration and plans to operate as a participant in DTC. If approved, its private, permissioned DLT rail would effectively become an alternate settlement track embedded within the existing U.S. central securities depository topology. Other DTC participants could access that track via their existing DTC connectivity, making PSSC a new node in the core financial market infrastructure (FMI) graph rather than an external experiment.
    • Bilateral DvP vs. multilateral netting and default management. The notice highlights that PSSC's service is designed for bilateral DvP settlement, in contrast to the multilateral netting and novation that underpin NSCC/DTC's current model. That raises structural questions the SEC now has 90 extra days to resolve: How would bilateral exposures interact with DTC's netted positions? How would margin, collateral, and default resources be structured? Could stresses in a private DLT book of bilateral trades propagate through PSSC's participant status into DTC and onward into other CCPs and banks?
    • Governance and ledger design as regulatory risk factors. Because PSSC's proposal centers on a private, permissioned distributed ledger, the usual ยง17A questions about safeguarding securities and funds now also implicate ledger governance: who controls validator sets, software upgrades, consensus parameters, key management, and incident response. The Commission's decision to invoke the 90-day extension explicitly to study this "novel structure" is a signal that DLT design choices are now first-class inputs into clearing-agency registration decisions.
    • A regulatory rate-limiter on DLT in systemically important plumbing. The extension does not decide the merits of PSSC's application, but it functions as a temporal gate on whether a DLT-based clearing node will be allowed to join the U.S. settlement core. An approval by May 3, 2026 could create a regulatory template for similar permissioned-ledger clearing services inside existing FMIs; a denial could signal a much narrower appetite for embedding bilateral DLT rails in systemically important clearing and settlement functions.

    Why this is structurally important and (very likely) under-covered

    This notice is framed as a routine scheduling action and spans a single Federal Register page. But the only reason to extend the deadline is that the Commission is still working through whether and how a DLT-native clearing agency can safely operate from inside DTC. That makes this one-page "longer-period" designation a control lever over the admissibility of distributed-ledger constructs in the core U.S. securities-settlement stack.

That, plus the highly technical framing, suggests this development is being tracked mainly by niche market-structure specialists, not general financial press.
