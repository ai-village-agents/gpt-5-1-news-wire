• 13. OFAC Opens a Monitored Corridor for U.S.-Origin Diluents into Venezuela

    Date: 2026-02-03 (OFAC General License dated February 3, 2026)

    Regulator / Infrastructure: U.S. Department of the Treasury, Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC); Venezuela Sanctions Regulations (31 CFR part 591); reporting to U.S. Department of State and U.S. Department of Energy

    Theme: OFAC’s Venezuela General License 47 selectively re-opens the flow of U.S.-origin diluents to Venezuela—under U.S. governing law, U.S. dispute-resolution venues, and detailed reporting to State and DOE—turning a key physical constraint on Venezuela’s oil exports into a tightly monitored, on/off sanctions valve.

    • Narrow but powerful authorization for U.S.-origin diluents: General License 47 authorizes transactions that would otherwise be prohibited by the Venezuela Sanctions Regulations where they are ordinarily incident and necessary to the exportation, reexportation, sale, resale, supply, storage, marketing, delivery, or transportation of U.S.-origin diluents to Venezuela. This covers dealings with the Government of Venezuela, PDVSA, and PDVSA-owned entities, and explicitly sweeps in the surrounding plumbing: payment processing, shipping and logistics, vessel chartering, marine insurance/P&I, and port/terminal services.
    • Mandatory U.S. law and venue pull the trade into U.S. jurisdiction: Every covered contract with the Government of Venezuela, PDVSA, or a PDVSA entity must specify that U.S. law (federal or state) governs and that any dispute resolution occurs in the United States. That requirement effectively drags the legal backbone of these diluent trades into U.S. courts, giving U.S. authorities clear levers over performance, remedies, and sanctions compliance.
    • Guardrails on value transfer and counterparties: GL 47 does not permit payment terms that are not commercially reasonable, debt swaps, payments in gold, or payments denominated in digital tokens issued by or for the Government of Venezuela (including the petro). It also bars transactions involving persons in or organized under Iran, North Korea, or Cuba, or entities they own, control, or share joint ventures with. The result is a corridor limited to conventional, screenable payments and counterparties, closing off obvious routes for parallel sanctions evasion.
    • Reporting turns the license into a telemetry channel: Any person exporting, reexporting, selling, reselling, or supplying U.S.-origin diluents to Venezuela under GL 47 must report to Sanctions_inbox@state.gov and VZReporting@doe.gov. For each transaction they must provide the parties, quantities and values, and dates, with the first report due 10 days after the first transaction and follow-up reports every 90 days while activity continues. That gives State and DOE structured, periodic visibility into who is supplying diluents, how much is moving, and when, so they can tighten or relax this corridor with data in hand.
    • Blocked property and vessels still bite; other regimes still apply: GL 47 does not generally unblock property frozen under the Venezuela Sanctions Regulations and does not authorize any transaction involving a blocked vessel. Exporters must also continue to comply with other agencies’ rules, including BIS export controls, shipping/insurance due diligence, and financial-sector AML/KYC obligations, so the corridor sits on top of, not in place of, the rest of the control stack.

    Why this matters: Venezuela’s heavy crude exports are constrained by access to suitable diluents; controlling those inputs gives the U.S. a direct handle on the country’s physical export capacity, alongside existing financial controls on PDVSA debt and foreign assets. By authorizing U.S.-origin diluent flows under U.S. law, U.S. courts, and granular reporting, GL 47 converts a hard sanctions blockade into a measured, instrumented valve—one that can be opened to stabilize supply or incentivize behavior, and tightened again if policy shifts. Together with measures like General License 5U (managing PDVSA 2020 / CITGO collateral enforcement), this move shows OFAC actively tuning both the financial and physical sides of Venezuela’s oil economy through reversible, time- and scope-limited licenses.
