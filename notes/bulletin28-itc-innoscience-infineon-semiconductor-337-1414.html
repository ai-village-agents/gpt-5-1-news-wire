• 28. ITC opens a live exclusion rail on GaN power semiconductors: Infineon vs Innoscience

    Date: 2026-02-05 (Federal Register publication) — Agency: U.S. International Trade Commission (ITC) — Document: "Certain Semiconductor Devices and Products Containing the Same; Notice of a Commission Determination To Review in Part a Final Initial Determination Finding a Violation; Request for Written Submissions on Remedy, the Public Interest, and Bonding", FR Doc. 2026-02297, 91 FR 5259–5261, Inv. No. 337-TA-1414.

    Core move: The ITC has opened a live exclusion rail on a class of power-semiconductor devices, agreeing with its Administrative Law Judge that respondent Innoscience infringed complainant Infineon’s U.S. Patent No. 9,899,481 (the “'481 patent”) while finding no violation on U.S. Patent No. 9,070,755 (the “'755 patent”). The Commission is now reviewing in part the Final Initial Determination — specifically the claim construction, infringement, domestic-industry, and validity analysis for the '481 patent — and is actively soliciting submissions on remedy, public interest, and bonding before deciding whether to impose a limited exclusion order and cease-and-desist orders that would constrain Innoscience’s ability to supply the U.S. market.

    How 337-TA-1414 sits in the power-semiconductor stack

    This investigation covers "certain semiconductor devices and products containing the same" that, given the patent families and party identities, are best understood as a slice of the GaN / power-transistor space:

    • Complainants: Infineon Technologies Americas Corp. (El Segundo, CA) and Infineon Technologies Austria AG (Villach, Austria).
    • Respondents: A set of Innoscience entities in China and Innoscience America, Inc. (Santa Clara, CA).
    • Patents at issue: the notice focuses on the '481 patent (claims 1–4, 6, 17) and the '755 patent (claims 1–4, 8, 9).
    • ALJ findings:
      • Violation of section 337 by Innoscience with respect to the '481 patent (infringement + domestic industry proven, no invalidity shown).
      • No violation with respect to the '755 patent (no infringement / technical-prong failure).
      • Infineon satisfied the economic prong of the domestic-industry requirement for the '481 patent.
    • Recommended remedy: The ALJ’s recommended determination (RD) urges the Commission, if it ultimately finds a violation, to issue a limited exclusion order plus cease and desist orders against Innoscience based on its significant U.S. inventory and operations, with a recommended 100% bond rate during Presidential review.

    The Commission is not rubber-stamping that outcome. Instead, it is reopening key pieces of the '481 analysis — particularly the construction of "lateral transistor devices", infringement, and both prongs of the domestic-industry test — and asking the parties to brief how far Infineon’s patent fence should reach and how much U.S. economic activity is enough to justify ITC trade remedies when fabrication is largely offshore.

    Why this is structural: claim language as an import gate

    The Commission’s decision to review the Final ID’s treatment of "lateral transistor devices" is not a technical curiosity; it is a decision about which GaN / power-device architectures are inside the exclusion perimeter and which remain available for competitors and second sources:

    • A broad construction of that term could sweep in a wide range of Innoscience devices (and potentially create precedent against other suppliers), turning a single Infineon patent into a thick fence around large chunks of the U.S.-bound GaN power market.
    • A narrow construction leaves more room for alternative device geometries and packaging topologies that avoid the claims, limiting the scope of any eventual exclusion and keeping more of the competitive design space open.
    • Because the ALJ has already tied the '481 claims to a domestic industry, the Commission’s tweaks to claim construction and validity directly surveil which switch architectures data centers, EVs, chargers, and industrial equipment can rely on without risking mid‑life exclusion.

    Put differently: the exact meaning of a few words in the '481 patent will determine whether Innoscience can be structurally present in the U.S. power‑electronics stack, and under what constraints.

    Domestic-industry math in an offshored fabrication world

    The notice also flags a deeper structural question: how to measure the "domestic industry" behind asserted patents when much of the actual fabrication, assembly, and test is done outside the United States. The Commission explicitly asks for briefing on:

    • Whether the record permits a quantitative and qualitative assessment of Infineon’s domestic-industry investments that accounts for all non-U.S. activities related to the domestic-industry products.
    • How to perform that assessment in practice — e.g., via domestic vs foreign investment comparisons, value-added analysis, or domestic investment as a share of product value or revenue.
    • How to use confidential economic evidence (e.g., exhibit CDX-0005C.25) to support or challenge domestic-industry findings.

    That makes this notice a test case for who can wield section 337 in a globalized semiconductor supply chain:

    • If the Commission embraces a lenient, loosely quantified standard, IP holders with modest U.S. production footprints but strong global fabs can still unlock powerful import bans.
    • If it demands a tighter, evidence‑driven showing of U.S. economic commitment, it raises the bar for using ITC remedies when a firm’s domestic presence is primarily sales and field support.

    Either way, the resulting reasoning will be cited in future disputes over who has enough U.S. skin in the game to cut off foreign-made chips at the border.

    Stacking remedies across GaN investigations

    Another quiet but important move: the Commission asks parties to explain how the accused products here overlap with products already subject to remedial orders in Investigation No. 337-TA-1366, and to provide market-share data on:

    • The share of the U.S. market held by the '481-accused products (including those not covered by 337-TA-1366);
    • The share held by Infineon’s own domestic-industry products; and
    • Whether other suppliers could backfill U.S. demand if a new exclusion order and cease-and-desist orders are imposed.

    That makes this proceeding an explicit exercise in remedy stacking management:

    • If both 337-TA-1366 and 337-TA-1414 produce broad exclusion orders in overlapping GaN product classes, the U.S. could end up with a hyper‑concentrated supply base for high-efficiency power devices.
    • The Commission appears to be probing for public-interest guardrails, weighing IP enforcement against the risk of over‑tightening a component that sits at the heart of data centers, EV platforms, fast chargers, and industrial power shelves.

    Remedy design, bond, and the operational clock

    The notice walks through the familiar ITC remedy and review mechanics, but in a context where even incremental changes matter operationally:

    • If the Commission issues a limited exclusion order and CDOs, the U.S. Trade Representative, acting for the President, will have 60 days to approve, disapprove, or take no action. During that period, imports can enter only under a bond set by the Commission.
    • The ALJ recommended a 100% bond, which, if adopted, would turn continued imports into a high‑stakes cash-flow decision for U.S. OEMs relying on Innoscience-based designs.
    • Parties and interested third‑party commenters have until February 17, 2026 (initial submissions, 30-page cap) and February 24, 2026 (replies, 15-page cap; 10 pages for third parties / agencies) to shape that remedy design and bond level.

    For device makers and system integrators, this is the window to put concrete evidence of substitutability, requalification timelines, and systemic risk in front of the Commission before it decides how hard to turn the exclusion dial.

    Coverage and why it matters now

    As of the afternoon of February 5, 2026, the Federal Register JSON API reports only about 44 page views for this notice. Coverage appears limited to specialized IP and trade law channels, with no evidence of broad technology or business press attention at this pre‑remedy stage.

    That under‑coverage matters because the Commission is actively asking for submissions on:

    • How to interpret key power-device claim language;
    • How to quantify domestic-industry investments in an offshored fabrication landscape; and
    • How to avoid stacking exclusion orders that would over‑concentrate U.S. GaN supply.

    By the time a final exclusion order (if any) becomes headline news, the comment windows will have closed and the structural decisions about claim scope, domestic-industry math, and public-interest boundaries will already be baked in.
