# FCC expedited pole-attachment complaint order (DOC-418491A1)

- **Primary source (news release):** `DOC-418491A1` – "FCC Issues Precedent-Setting Order Under Expedited Review Process for Pole Attachment Complaints" (Feb. 5, 2026).
  - Local mirror: `sources/fcc-20260205-pole-attachment-expedited-order-doc-418491a1.txt`.
  - FCC EDOCS page: https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-issues-order-under-expedited-pole-attachment-complaint-process
  - News release text also links to the underlying Memorandum Opinion and Order: https://www.fcc.gov/document/rapid-broadband-assessment-team-resolves-pole-attachment-complaint

## What the FCC actually did

- Announced a **first-of-its-kind order** issued under the FCC's **expedited review process for pole attachment complaints**, using the Commission's **Accelerated Docket** procedures.
- Featured the work of the **Rapid Broadband Assessment Team (RBAT)**, composed of staff from the **Enforcement Bureau** and **Wireline Competition Bureau**.
- RBAT oversaw development of a record including both parties' legal arguments and delivered an order **within 60 days** of complaint filing.
- The dispute involved **Appalachian Power Company (APCO)** and **Comcast**, focused on **cost responsibility for pole replacements** in cases where:
  - APCO's pole had a **preexisting violation** of safety or engineering standards requiring replacement, and
  - Comcast sought to use the pole for broadband deployment under pole-attachment rules.
- APCO's position: Comcast should pay the **full cost** of such replacements when it wanted to use the pole.
- FCC's clarified position: under Commission rules, **Comcast only has to pay the incremental cost** of installing a stronger or taller pole than the one that would otherwise be used to correct the previous third-party violation.
- The dispute was not abstract: APCO's policy had **slowed and increased costs for BEAD-funded broadband projects in Virginia**.

## Structural significance

- **Expedited enforcement rails for pole disputes**
  - This is a **precedent-setting** use of the FCC's expedited review / Accelerated Docket process for pole-attachment complaints.
  - RBAT is operationalized as a **cross-bureau enforcement/mediation rail**, not just a paper policy.
  - A 60-day turnaround becomes a **de facto SLA** for at least some pole-attachment disputes, shifting expectations of how quickly utilities and attachers can get resolution.

- **Rebalancing utility vs. attacher leverage in the BEAD era**
  - By rejecting the idea that attachers like Comcast must bear **full replacement costs** when a pole already had third-party violations, the FCC reinforces the principle that utilities cannot use legacy violations to **externalize their own upgrade costs** onto new broadband builds.
  - This materially affects the economics of **BEAD-funded** projects, where pole replacement and make-ready costs can dominate budgets and timelines.
  - The decision signals that utilities' internal safety/engineering debt **cannot simply be shifted** onto federally funded broadband deployments.

- **Clarifying cost allocation as a control-plane rule**
  - The core clarification—**incremental cost only**, not full replacement cost—acts as a **routing rule for capital**:
    - Preexisting violations remain a utility responsibility.
    - Only the incremental stronger/taller pole attributable to the new attachment is chargeable to the attacher.
  - This is not just a private settlement; it is framed as a Commission order rooted in existing rules, which can be cited by other attachers.

- **RBAT as a standing institution**
  - The release leans on the narrative that **RBAT and the Accelerated Docket** are now "standing by" to resolve future disputes.
  - Structurally, this creates a **credible threat** that utilities' cost-allocation or delay-inducing policies can be brought to a fast-track FCC forum.
  - Over time, the existence of RBAT + Accelerated Docket may push utilities toward **ex-ante compliance** with FCC cost and timing rules rather than testing aggressive policies in bilateral negotiations.

- **Integration with the FCC's "Build America Agenda"**
  - The release explicitly ties this order to a broader **Build America Agenda**:
    - Unleashing high-speed infrastructure builds.
    - Advancing national security and the space economy.
    - Strengthening tower and telecom workforce.
    - Pursuing a "massive and comprehensive deregulatory agenda".
  - That framing positions pole-attachment enforcement as **central infrastructure policy**, not merely a rate dispute.
