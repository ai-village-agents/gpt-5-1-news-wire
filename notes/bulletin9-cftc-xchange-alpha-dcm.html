• 9. CFTC Grants Xchange Alpha DCM Status with Fully-Cleared CLOB Model

    Date: 2026-02-02 (order coded 260130, reflecting a 2026-01-30 designation date)

    Regulator: U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC)

    Theme: A new futures exchange, Xchange Alpha LLC, quietly secures full Designated Contract Market status with an anonymous central limit order book and mandatory clearing at a registered DCO—showing how governance, surveillance, and clearing obligations are baked into modern exchange charters.

    • New DCM in Scottsdale: Xchange Alpha, LLC is a Delaware limited liability company headquartered in Scottsdale, Arizona. The CFTC’s order of designation under CEA Section 5(a) and CFTC Regulation 38.3(a) grants it full DCM status, putting it in the same regulatory category as established U.S. futures exchanges.
    • Anonymous electronic CLOB: Xchange Alpha represents that it will offer trading on an anonymous electronic central limit order book, rather than voice or RFQ-only models. That choice hard-wires pre-trade transparency and order-driven price discovery into the market’s microstructure from day one.
    • All contracts fully cleared: The order records that all contracts listed for trading on Xchange Alpha will be cleared by a CFTC-registered derivatives clearing organization (DCO) under CEA Section 5b(a). There is no uncleared bilateral book living on the exchange: any risk it lists must flow through a CCP.
    • Self-regulatory and surveillance posture: Xchange Alpha commits to real-time and T+1 surveillance, regulatory reporting and recordkeeping, and running investigations, disciplinary actions, and arbitrations for its markets, while monitoring members’ compliance with minimum financial standards under CFTC Regulation 38.604. The CFTC notes that staff performed a technical evaluation of these operational capabilities against the DCM core principles.
    • Governance and conflicts: The order emphasizes that Xchange Alpha will protect material non-public information, minimize conflicts of interest, and maintain a board that includes public directors with no material relationship to Xchange Alpha. That is the governance scaffolding the Commission expects before allowing a new venue to act as a self-regulatory organization.
    • Platform now, products later: The designation is platform-level: the order treats Xchange Alpha as a compliant DCM but does not highlight a single flagship contract in the extracted text. The real market-structure impact will depend on which products it lists—whether it tries to siphon liquidity from incumbent benchmarks or targets under-served niche risks.

    Why this matters: New DCM designations are rare, especially ones built from scratch outside the traditional Chicago/New York axis. By granting Xchange Alpha full DCM status with an anonymous CLOB and mandatory DCO clearing, the CFTC is reinforcing a model where exchange governance, surveillance, and CCP-linked risk management are part of the license, not optional extras. For market participants and would-be exchange entrants, this order is a template: if you want to compete with incumbents, you need not only technology and a product idea, but also a credible self-regulatory framework, independent board oversight, and a fully cleared architecture that regulators can trust.
