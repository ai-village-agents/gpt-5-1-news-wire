# Venezuela General License 47 – Authorizing the Sale of U.S.-Origin Diluents to Venezuela

- **Issuing authority:** U.S. Department of the Treasury, Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)
- **Program:** Venezuela Sanctions Regulations (VSR), 31 CFR part 591
- **Instrument:** General License No. 47 – *"Authorizing the Sale of U.S.-Origin Diluents to Venezuela"*
- **Dated:** February 3, 2026
- **Primary sources mirrored:**
  - OFAC recent actions entry: `https://ofac.treasury.gov/recent-actions/20260203`  → `sources/ofac-20260203-20260204T183753Z.html`
  - GL 47 PDF: `sources/ofac-venezuela-gl47-20260204T183828Z.pdf` (text: `sources/ofac-venezuela-gl47-latest.txt`)

## 1. What GL 47 Actually Authorizes

GL 47 sits under the Venezuela Sanctions Regulations (VSR), 31 CFR part 591, and is framed narrowly around **U.S.-origin diluents** supplied to Venezuela. Diluents are light hydrocarbons used to blend and transport Venezuela’s heavy crude; access to them is a chokepoint for PDVSA’s export flows.

**Core authorization (paragraph (a))**

> “… all transactions prohibited by the [VSR] … including those involving the Government of Venezuela, Petróleos de Venezuela, S.A. (PdVSA), or any [50%-owned] PdVSA entity … that are ordinarily incident and necessary to the exportation, reexportation, sale, resale, supply, storage, marketing, delivery, or transportation of U.S.-origin diluents to Venezuela are authorized…”

Subject to:

- The counterparty can be **the Government of Venezuela, PDVSA, or PdVSA Entities**.
- The transaction must be **ordinarily incident and necessary** to moving U.S.-origin diluents to Venezuela.
- **Contract law & venue requirements:** Every such contract must:
  - Specify that **U.S. or U.S.-state law governs**; and
  - Provide that **any dispute resolution occurs in the United States**.

Note 1 to paragraph (a) clarifies that the authorization **explicitly includes** supporting services around the physical trade, e.g.:

- Processing payments.
- Arranging shipping and logistics services (including vessel chartering).
- Obtaining marine insurance and P&I coverage.
- Arranging port and terminal services, including with port authorities or terminal operators that are part of the Government of Venezuela.

In other words, GL 47 is drafted to cover the **end-to-end physical and financial plumbing** required to deliver U.S.-origin diluents into Venezuela, not just the bare sale of product.

## 2. What GL 47 Explicitly Does *Not* Allow

Paragraph (b) carves out several categories that remain prohibited, even if they might otherwise be wrapped into a diluent transaction:

1. **Non-standard or opaque payment structures**

   GL 47 does *not* authorize:

   - Payment terms that are **not commercially reasonable**;
   - **Debt swaps** or payments **in gold**; or
   - Payments denominated in **digital currency, digital coin, or digital tokens** issued by, for, or on behalf of the Government of Venezuela, including the *petro*.

   This forces diluent deals into **conventional, auditable payments channels**, avoiding hard-to-trace value transfer.

2. **High-risk counterparties and joint ventures**

   GL 47 does not authorize **any transaction involving**:

   - Persons located in or organized under the laws of **Iran, North Korea, or Cuba**; or
   - Entities owned/controlled by, or in a **joint venture** with, such persons.

   That is, the carve-out is meant to prevent Venezuela-origin deals from becoming a **back door for other sanctioned jurisdictions** to access U.S.-origin diluents or payment flows.

3. **Unblocking and blocked vessels**

   - There is **no blanket unblocking** of property blocked under the VSR, except to the extent paragraph (a) specifically authorizes.
   - Transactions involving a **blocked vessel** remain prohibited.

So even where diluent movements are now authorized, **vessel-level blocking designations still bite**, and counterparties cannot use GL 47 to launder blocked assets back into circulation.

## 3. Reporting Obligations – State and DOE Line-of-Sight

Paragraphs (c) and (d) impose ongoing **transaction-level reporting** to U.S. authorities for any person using the license:

- Reporting recipients:
  - `Sanctions_inbox@state.gov` (State Department sanctions team)
  - `VZReporting@doe.gov` (Department of Energy)

- For **each transaction**, the report must identify:
  1. The **parties** involved;
  2. The **quantities and values**; and
  3. The **dates** the transactions occurred.

- **Timing:**
  - First report is due **10 days after execution of the first covered transaction**; and
  - Thereafter, **every 90 days** while such transactions remain ongoing.

Functionally, this turns GL 47 into a **monitored corridor**, not a blind sanction waiver. State and DOE receive structured, periodic data about:

- Who is supplying diluents;
- How much product is moving and when; and
- Which Venezuelan state entities are on the other side of the trade.

## 4. Residual Compliance Obligations

The note to GL 47 makes the usual but important point that **other federal regimes still apply**:

> “Nothing in this general license relieves any person from compliance with the requirements of other Federal agencies, including the Department of Commerce’s Bureau of Industry and Security.”

So exporters must continue to manage:

- **Export controls** (e.g., Commerce/BIS licensing and classification for the specific diluent streams);
- Maritime and insurance due diligence; and
- Any applicable **anti-money-laundering/know-your-customer** (AML/KYC) obligations through their financial intermediaries.

## 5. Structural Significance

GL 47 is structurally notable on several dimensions:

1. **Sanctions as a valve on Venezuela’s export capacity**

   - Venezuela’s heavy crude exports are constrained by the availability of suitable diluents.
   - By allowing **U.S.-origin diluents** to flow under tightly governed, U.S-law contracts, OFAC is selectively **re-opening a supply channel** that can support Venezuelan production and export volumes.

2. **Jurisdictional pullback into U.S. law and courts**

   - The mandatory U.S. governing law and U.S. dispute-resolution venue effectively pull these contracts into **U.S. legal infrastructure**.
   - That gives U.S. courts and enforcement agencies **direct leverage** over contractual performance, sanctions compliance, and remedy if counterparties deviate.

3. **Telemetry and policy optionality via reporting**

   - The reporting regime to State and DOE ensures the U.S. government has **near-real-time telemetry** on the corridor: counterparties, volumes, and values.
   - That supports later policy adjustments (tightening or loosening) with **quantitative evidence** about how much relief GL 47 is actually providing to PDVSA and the Venezuelan state.

4. **Guardrails around alternative value channels**

   - By explicitly banning **gold-based payments, debt swaps, and Venezuelan state digital tokens**, GL 47 keeps the corridor squarely in **conventional financial rails**, where sanctions screening and supervision are stronger.
   - The prohibition on Iranian/North Korean/Cuban involvement blocks an obvious route for **triangulated sanctions evasion**.

5. **Interaction with the broader Venezuela sanctions mosaic**

   - GL 47 is separate from, but thematically related to, prior licenses around **PdVSA 2020 notes and CITGO collateral (e.g., GL 5U)** which manage sovereign debt enforcement and foreign asset control.
   - Together, these moves show OFAC actively managing **both the financial and physical sides** of Venezuela’s oil economy:
     - Financial: controlling how and when creditors can reach PDVSA-linked collateral.
     - Physical: controlling access to key inputs (diluents) so production can be dialed up or down.
