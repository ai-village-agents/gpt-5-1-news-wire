  • 11. SEC Lets DTC Tie Net Debit Caps to Liquidity, Raising the Ceiling on U.S. Equity Settlement

    Date: 2026-02-03 (Federal Register publication; SEC order dated January 29, 2026)

    Regulator / Infrastructure: U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC); The Depository Trust Company (DTC) central securities depository and settlement system

    Theme: The SEC approves a DTC rule change that replaces fixed Net Debit Caps with caps that scale against DTC’s qualifying liquid resources, quietly rewriting how much intraday settlement risk the core U.S. securities plumbing can carry.

    • From hard cap to function of liquidity: DTC’s Net Debit Caps—historically set at $2.15B per participant and $2.85B per affiliated family—will no longer be static numbers. Under the approved rule change, caps will instead be set “based on DTC’s liquidity resources, related costs, and projected benefits to Participants,” and must always sit below DTC’s total qualifying liquid resources.
    • Qualifying liquid resources behind the cap: The order describes DTC’s current qualifying liquid resources as a $1.15B Participants Fund plus a $1.9B committed line of credit—about $3.05B in total—supplemented going forward by up to $3B of senior notes under an already approved debt-issuance programme. Net Debit Caps will now be tuned explicitly against this pool, rather than a fixed $2.15B / $2.85B ceiling.
    • Fewer emergency wires, smoother throughput: A confidential Impact Study, summarised in the order, indicates that increasing caps by roughly $0.75–$1B would have cut daily Settlement Progress Payments (SPPs) by about $3.62–$4.43B across 17 large participant families over the study period—evidence that higher, liquidity-linked caps can reduce recycling and last‑minute funding maneuvers.
    • Who funds the Liquidity Fund now shifts: The Liquidity Fund portion of the Participants Fund was previously paid only by affiliated families with caps above $2.15B. With caps now able to exceed that level for standalone firms, unaffiliated participants whose caps cross $2.15B will be pulled into Liquidity Fund contributions, redistributing the cost of shared settlement liquidity.
    • Affiliated family governance tightened: The Settlement Guide adds formal definitions for Affiliated Family and Unaffiliated Participant, clarifies how an aggregate family Net Debit Cap is shared among members, and amends Rule 9(B) so that no transaction may process if it would push an affiliated family over its aggregate cap—regardless of which entity is instructing the trade.

    Why this matters: Net Debit Caps are one of DTC’s main throttles on intraday settlement risk. Turning them into a parameterised function of qualifying liquid resources—backed by prefunded funds, credit lines, and new senior notes—effectively makes the maximum load of the U.S. equity settlement system a tunable policy choice rather than a fixed number. At the same time, pulling very large standalone firms into the Liquidity Fund and formalising “affiliated family” definitions reshapes who pays for, and who is constrained by, that shared liquidity pool. Market participants should treat this as a structural change in the control plane of U.S. securities clearance and settlement, not a housekeeping edit.

  • 12. FICC and CME Seek to Move Customer U.S. Treasury Cross-Margining Into Futures Law

    Date: 2026-02-03 (Federal Register publication; SEC notice dated January 30, 2026)

    Regulator / Infrastructure: U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC); Fixed Income Clearing Corporation (FICC) government securities division; Chicago Mercantile Exchange Inc. (CME) derivatives clearing organization

    Theme: FICC and CME ask the SEC for Section 36 exemptive relief so that certain customers can cross-margin U.S. Treasury securities and related futures in a single futures account, shifting those customers from the stockbroker/SIPA regime into the futures/commodity-broker regime for a defined slice of their Treasury activity.

    • From house-only to customer cross-margining: FICC and CME already run a proprietary cross-margining arrangement that lets a joint broker-dealer / futures commission merchant (BD–FCM) have its own Treasury cash and futures books margined as one portfolio. The new Application asks to extend that framework to Eligible Customers of an Eligible BD–FCM so their cleared Treasury securities at FICC and Treasury/interest-rate futures at CME can be cross-margined together.
    • Key exemption: moving designated customer Treasuries into a futures account: To enable customer cross-margining, FICC and CME ask the SEC to exempt Eligible BD–FCMs from Exchange Act Section 15(c)(3) and the Customer Protection Rule (Rule 15c3-3) for a narrow set of positions. Specifically, certain customer U.S. Treasury securities and related margin that support the cross-margined portfolio would be held in a CEA Section 4d futures account and treated under CFTC segregation rules and Part 190 commodity-broker liquidation, rather than in a securities account governed by Rule 15c3-3 and SIPA.
    • Opt-in and disclosure, not stealth re-labelling: Cross-margining under the “Proposed Customer XM Framework” is voluntary: it only applies if both the Eligible Customer and its Eligible BD–FCM choose to participate, and only for positions that are already Eligible Positions under the existing proprietary arrangement. Before taking any property, the BD–FCM must give the customer a disclosure document explaining that (a) the assets will be held in a futures account with subchapter IV protections, and (b) the broker-dealer segregation rules, SIPA customer status, and stockbroker liquidation provisions will not apply to that property.
    • Non-conforming subordination agreement rewires bankruptcy treatment: Each participating customer (an “XM Customer”) must sign a non-conforming subordination agreement acknowledging that its cross-margined Treasury securities and associated FICC-held margin will not be treated as “customer” property under the Exchange Act or SIPA, and that any SIPA/stockbroker claims tied to those assets will be subordinated to the claims of all other customers. In return, the XM Customer relies on the futures customer protections of CEA Section 4d(a)(2) and Part 190.
    • Risk model and governance stay at the CCP layer: FICC and CME commit to continue calculating initial margin for XM Customer Positions on a gross, customer-by-customer basis using the same risk-offsetting methodology as in the house program. They also agree to amend their rulebooks and service guides as needed, and to require each Eligible BD–FCM to collect at least the aggregate initial margin demanded by each clearinghouse and stay in compliance with capital, liquidity, and segregation rules.

    Why this matters: Treasury clearing reforms have pushed more activity into central clearing but left open how customers can efficiently manage risk across cash Treasuries and futures. This Application sketches a path to customer-level cross-margining that could lower margin costs and encourage more cleared Treasury use, but it does so by deliberately moving a subset of sophisticated customers out of the broker-dealer/SIPA protection stack and into the futures/commodity-broker stack. If the SEC and CFTC both grant the requested exemptions—potentially on terms broad enough for other clearing agency/DCO pairs—this would mark a structural shift in how U.S. Treasury market risk, customer protections, and default management are shared between securities CCPs and futures CCPs.

    Date: 2026-02-04 (Federal Register publication; SEC filing SR-FICC-2026-003)
    Domain: CCP risk management / U.S. Treasury & repo clearing (FICC GSD)
    Theme: From hard activity limits to liquidity-sensitive margin overlays for sponsored and agent-cleared clients.

    • Old regime – capital-based activity brake: Under GSD Rule 3A, Section 2(h), if the sum of VaR Charges on a Sponsoring Member's omnibus and dealer accounts (“Aggregate VaR Charges”) exceeded its Netting Member Capital, the member was barred from submitting additional Sponsored activity unless FICC overrode the cap.     • Liquidity-triggered “higher-of” VaR overlay for giant indirects: For each Sponsored Member or Segregated Indirect Participant, FICC will aggregate across all sponsors and agent clearers the sum of Receive Obligations and Funds-Only Settlement Amounts. If, on any day, that sum exceeds FICC's Daily Liquidity Need, then for the next 25 business days FICC applies the higher of the start-of-day (prior EOD) and intraday VaR Charges as the intraday VaR for that indirect participant, via the relevant Sponsoring Member or Agent Clearing Member segregated accounts.
    • Scope and exclusions: The “higher-of” methodology is extended to Segregated Indirect Participant Accounts but is not applied to non-segregated Agent Clearing Member Omnibus Accounts, which remain net-margined across executing customers. A technical correction in the Margin Component Schedule clarifies that the overlay applies to the VaR Charge, not the entire Required Fund Deposit.
    • Impact study – margin redistributed, not universally increased: In a study covering April 1, 2024–October 31, 2025 (398 business days, 696 Sponsored Members with relevant activity), FICC finds that 665 Sponsored Members (~95.5%) would see lower VaR because the always-on “higher-of” surcharge is removed for them, with an average daily reduction of about $20.2m (~32%). Only five Sponsored Members remain in the liquidity-triggered “higher-of” regime, facing an average daily VaR increase of about $144.6m (~19%), with the largest single-day uplifts on the order of $680m–$830m (≈40–52% increases) on those days.
    • Access vs. safeguards: Deleting the activity limit removes a coarse brake that could abruptly shut down sponsored activity once a sponsor's Aggregate VaR surpassed its capital, and instead relies on: (a) general risk tools under Rules 3 and 21 to constrain problematic members, and (b) a targeted liquidity-based margin overlay for indirect participants whose flows exceed FICC's Daily Liquidity Need.     Date: 2026-02-04 (Federal Register proposed regulations)
    Regulator / Infrastructure: U.S. Department of the Treasury and Internal Revenue Service; Section 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit; elective payment and transferability under sections 6417 and 6418
    Theme: Treasury and IRS propose detailed rules that turn Section 45Z into a performance-indexed production credit for transportation fuels, tying dollar payouts to lifecycle emissions against a statutory baseline while locking eligible projects into North American supply chains and non-foreign-controlled ownership structures.

    • Credit mechanics – emissions factor times an “applicable amount”: Section 45Z sets a statutory baseline lifecycle emissions rate of 50 kg CO₂e per mmBTU. A fuel’s emissions factor is the fractional improvement versus that baseline, calculated as (50 − emissions rate) ÷ 50 and rounded to the nearest 0.1. The proposed regulations apply this factor to a per-gallon applicable amount to determine the credit: roughly, 45Z credit = applicable amount × gallons × emissions factor. With prevailing wage and apprenticeship (PWA) requirements met, the alternative amount reaches about $1.00/gal for non‑SAF fuels and $1.75/gal for SAF through 2025, inflation‑indexed thereafter, creating a linear carbon‑performance scale embedded in the tax code.
    • Lifecycle emissions tables as a live control surface: Emissions rates (in kg CO₂e/mmBTU) come either from an annual emissions rate table the Secretary publishes for types of transportation fuel, or from a Provisional Emissions Rate (PER) determination. Those rates must be based on lifecycle greenhouse gas methodology tied to Clean Air Act §211(o)(1)(H), and may not be less than zero for post‑2025 production (except certain animal‑manure fuels). Because the emissions factor – and thus the size of the credit – depends directly on these tables, Treasury’s lifecycle modeling choices become a recurring policy lever for steering capital toward or away from specific fuel pathways without changing the statutory per‑gallon amounts.
    • Eligibility, geography, and foreign‑influence filters: To claim 45Z, the taxpayer must produce transportation fuel in the United States (including territories), be registered as a producer under section 4101, and sell the fuel to an unrelated person in a qualified sale. For fuel produced after December 31, 2025, the feedstock must be produced or grown in the U.S., Mexico, or Canada, effectively constraining subsidized supply chains to North America. In addition, taxpayers that are specified foreign entities are barred for tax years beginning after July 4, 2025, and those that are broader foreign‑influenced entities (with a narrow statutory exception) are barred for years beginning after July 4, 2027. The result is a clean‑fuel production subsidy that is explicitly conditioned on both geography and ownership/control, turning eligibility into an industrial‑policy and national‑security filter.
    • Anti‑stacking with 45V, 45Q, and 48 makes credit identity a design decision: The regulations define a “qualified facility” as one used to produce transportation fuels but exclude any facility that, in the same year, claims (via section 38) the 45V clean hydrogen credit, the relevant portion of the section 48 energy credit through a 48(a)(15) election on a specified clean hydrogen facility, or the 45Q carbon oxide sequestration credit. The preamble groups these as “anti‑stacking credits”. Project sponsors therefore must choose whether a facility lives under **45Z** or under **45V/45Q/48**, and that choice determines which regime governs its cash‑flow profile and default assumptions. This forces clean‑energy developers, tax‑equity investors, and offtakers to treat “credit identity” as a core structural design parameter for each site rather than layering multiple IRA credits on the same production.
    • Two‑layer governance of production vs monetization: The proposed rules emphasize that every business with a separate EIN (including disregarded entities and QSubs) must be separately registered under section 4101 as a producer of clean fuel, aligning 45Z registration with existing excise‑tax regimes. By contrast, elections for elective payment (section 6417) or credit transferability (section 6418) are made by the upstream taxpayer that owns those entities, not by the disregarded facility‑level entity itself. That split creates a two‑layer control plane: the IRS can supervise operational compliance and measurement at the facility level via 4101 registration, while separately overseeing how consolidated groups cash out or sell the resulting 45Z streams via 6417/6418. In effect, lifecycle‑weighted carbon performance at a plant is being turned into a portable, financeable asset at the group level.
    • Timing, common storage, and trade flows: The credit is available only in the tax year of the qualified sale, not the year of production, and is disallowed for transportation fuel produced before January 1, 2025. Detailed examples show how gallons from multiple facilities commingled in common storage must be allocated pro rata back to each facility’s production for credit purposes, with their respective emissions factors and PWA status. This preserves the link between physical production, lifecycle performance, and the location and employment conditions of the facility, even when actual supply chains are pooled in tanks and pipelines.

    Why this matters: These regulations operationalize Section 45Z as a sliding‑scale carbon price for transportation fuels, paid out through the tax system. By tying the credit linearly to lifecycle emissions versus a fixed baseline, and then overlaying North American feedstock rules, foreign‑influence exclusions, and anti‑stacking with 45V/45Q/48, Treasury and IRS are defining not just who gets a subsidy, but which production geographies, ownership structures, and technology stacks will anchor the next decade of clean‑fuel infrastructure. At the same time, the split between facility‑level 4101 registration and group‑level 6417/6418 elections turns these performance‑weighted credits into financial instruments that can be monetized or transferred, while keeping the lifecycle‑emissions tables themselves as a live lever for future policy adjustments.

  • 18. Cboe EDGX turns ERSTP "Unique Identifier" into a beneficial-ownership control knob

    Date: 2026-02-04 — Venue / Regulator: Cboe EDGX Exchange, Inc.; U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) — Document: Self-Regulatory Organizations; Cboe EDGX Exchange, Inc.; Notice of Filing and Immediate Effectiveness of a Proposed Rule Change To Amend Exchange Rule 11.10(d) (EdgeRisk Self Trade Prevention (ERSTP) Modifiers) To Revise the Definition of Unique Identifier (91 Fed. Reg. 5145–5149, FR Doc. 2026-02227; Release No. 34-104754; File No. SR–CboeEDGX–2026–005).

    Core move: EDGX keeps its EdgeRisk Self Trade Prevention (ERSTP) mechanics but rewrites what counts as a Unique Identifier. Instead of a closed list (MPID, Member ID, ERSTP Group, affiliate ID, Multiple Access ID), Rule 11.10(d) now lets EDGX create a Unique Identifier at (i) the MPID level, (ii) the firm level, or (iii) wherever a User attests that ERSTP is needed to prevent transactions that do not change beneficial ownership. Self-trade prevention is tied directly to beneficial ownership, not just plumbing IDs.

    Mechanics

    • ERSTP basics unchanged: ERSTP remains an optional risk tool. An incoming order marked with an ERSTP modifier will not execute against a resting opposite-side order that is also ERSTP-marked with the same Unique Identifier. The modifier on the incoming order still determines the outcome (cancel incoming, cancel resting, cancel both, cancel smallest, or size-adjust).
    • Old Unique Identifier taxonomy: Under the prior Rule 11.10(d), a Unique Identifier was any of: (1) MPID; (2) Exchange Member identifier; (3) ERSTP Group identifier; (4) affiliate identifier (based on >50% ownership on Form BD plus an affidavit); or (5) Multiple Access identifier (for firms that both connect directly and via Sponsored Access). These constructs anchored ERSTP to technical connectivity and formal ownership records.
    • New three-path definition: The amended rule says a Unique Identifier may now be created at:
      • (i) MPID level — same as before: ERSTP scoped to a single market participant identifier.
      • (ii) Firm level — includes Exchange Member identifiers and ERSTP Group identifiers that span multiple MPIDs or desks.
      • (iii) Beneficial-ownership level — “where the User indicates that ERSTP is necessary in order to prevent transactions in securities in which there is no change in beneficial ownership” and completes an Exchange-provided attestation.
      The text explicitly drops the named affiliate identifier and Multiple Access identifier, but says the new definition still captures those concepts.
    • Why EDGX needed more flexibility: The filing describes cases where the old taxonomy breaks:
      • Firms that route different strategies or time zones through distinct Sponsored Participants and sponsoring brokers (e.g., a U.S. desk via Sponsoring Member 1 and a European desk via Sponsoring Member 2) but want ERSTP across both because they share a single beneficial owner.
      • Structures where a parent firm uses both a direct EDGX membership and multiple Sponsored Access pathways, potentially via different intermediaries, such that there is no single MPID or Sponsored Participant code that spans all flows.
      Under the old rule, EDGX could struggle to justify a single Unique Identifier; under item (iii), it can create one keyed to the economic owner.
    • Attestation and responsibility: Any User asking for a Unique Identifier under new item (iii) must first complete an Exchange attestation that ERSTP is needed to prevent trades with no change in beneficial ownership. EDGX stresses that ERSTP is a supplemental tool: firms remain fully responsible for complying with Exchange Act Section 9(a)(1) (wash sales) and FINRA Rules 6140 and 5210 on self-trades and false prints, and cannot rely solely on ERSTP to satisfy supervisory obligations.
    • Timing and process: The change is filed as a Rule 19b–4(f)(6) “non-controversial” rule change. The Commission waives the 30-day operative delay, making it effective upon filing (Jan. 28, 2026), with EDGX planning to implement in Q1 2026 and announce the go-live via Trade Desk Notice.

    Why this matters (structural view)

    • From pipes to people: beneficial ownership as the key: The old ERSTP model tied self-trade prevention to plumbing identifiers (MPIDs, Member IDs, Sponsored Participant codes, affiliate flags). The new model lets a firm (and EDGX) define the perimeter in terms of who actually owns the risk. That moves the core question from “are these orders on the same pipe?” to “are these orders from the same beneficial owner?”
    • Global firms can centralize self-trade controls across brokers: Large cross-border firms often fragment their connectivity across regions and sponsoring brokers. The attestation-based Unique Identifier lets them ask EDGX to stitch those paths together so that two desks in different time zones, using different sponsoring brokers, still cannot print against each other if they roll up to the same economic owner. That turns ERSTP into a venue-level beneficial-ownership firewall for their own flow.
    • Cleaner alignment with wash sale and self-trade doctrines: By grounding the new path explicitly in “no change in beneficial ownership,” EDGX is aligning its self-trade controls with the legal concepts that underpin wash sale and self-trade prohibitions. Firms can point to ERSTP Unique Identifiers as part of their program for preventing manipulative or misleading prints, while regulators can treat the attestation trail as evidence if those controls are abused.
    • Attestations as a governance lever, not just paperwork: The mandated Exchange attestation for item (iii) gives EDGX and the SEC a hook if a firm uses wide ERSTP scopes to avoid interacting with competitors rather than to stop true self-trades. Because the firm must represent that the relevant flows share beneficial ownership, the attestation becomes part of the evidence record in any later enforcement about unfair discrimination or spoofed liquidity.
    • Impact on the tape and surveillance: As more firms use ERSTP at a granular beneficial-ownership level, some self-trade prints disappear from the consolidated tape and EDGX market data. That can reduce noise from unintentional self-trades and highlight remaining patterns where self-trades still occur despite available tools, making those cases structurally more suspicious for surveillance teams.
    • Template for multi-venue harmonization: This filing sits alongside similar 2026 Cboe BZX/BYX/EDGA STP/MTP changes. Together, they push toward a harmonized, ownership-aware concept of “same actor” across Cboe U.S. equity markets. Competing venues that still tie STP narrowly to a single MPID or membership ID may face pressure to match this flexibility or justify why cross-broker self-trade prevention is harder on their books.
