  • 10. CFTC Rewires Large Trader Reporting Timeline with Part 17 No-Action Letter

    Date: 2026-01-27 (staff letter and press release; implementation calls begin Feb 18, 2026)

    Regulator: U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) – Division of Market Oversight (DMO) and Division of Data (DOD)

    Theme: CFTC uses a no-action letter plus technical guidebooks and a new Portal to turn the 2024 Part 17 large trader reporting rule into a staged implementation program, pushing effective enforcement out to mid-2027 while upgrading its market-surveillance telemetry.

    • Part 17 Final Rule baseline: The 2024 Large Trader Reporting Requirements final rule (89 Fed. Reg. 47439) modernizes Part 17 futures and options position reporting, expanding data elements and standardizing submissions in a structured FIXML format. The rule set a formal compliance date of June 3, 2026.
    • FIA request for more runway: On December 12, 2025, the Futures Industry Association (FIA) asked DMO to extend the compliance date, citing three missing building blocks: (1) staff implementation calls with industry, (2) a publicly testable updated CFTC Portal, and (3) a finalized revised Part 17 Guidebook.
    • No-action letter 26-02 resets the clock: In Staff Letter No. 26-02 (January 27, 2026), DMO interprets FIA’s request as a plea for no-action relief and agrees that it will not recommend enforcement action for failure to comply with the Part 17 Final Rule until after CFTC staff have (1) started implementation calls, (2) opened the new Portal for public testing, and (3) published a revised Guidebook, followed by an additional 18-month no-action window.
    • Guidebook v2.0 and FIXML implementation: In parallel, the Division of Data publishes Version 2.0 of the Part 17 Guidebook (December 17, 2024) and launches a dedicated Part 17 FIXML Implementation page, which together define the data dictionary, message structure, and submission mechanics for large trader reports into the updated CFTC Portal.
    • New enforcement horizon: July 26, 2027: Press Release 9174-26 explains that DMO and DOD now expect reporting firms (FCMs, clearing members, foreign brokers, and DCMs) to be in compliance with the Part 17 Final Rule by around July 26, 2027—transforming what was a single statutory date into a sequenced program: Portal and documentation first, then 18 months of testing and migration.
    • Surveillance and control-plane implications: By conditioning enforcement on its own technical readiness and offering a fixed 18-month runway once key infrastructure is live, the CFTC is treating data standards, portals, and implementation guides as first-class regulatory instruments. Large trader reporting becomes a programmable control plane: staff letters and guidebooks determine when and how the Commission actually sees concentrated positions across markets, and firms can plan systems work against a clear, infrastructure-driven horizon.

    Why this matters: Large trader reports are one of the CFTC’s main surveillance feeds for spotting concentrated risk and abusive trading across futures and options markets. The 2024 Part 17 overhaul is a major upgrade of that telemetry, but DMO’s no-action letter makes clear that surveillance quality now depends on the maturity of the Commission’s own tooling as much as on rule text. This is a template for future data-heavy regulations: publish the rule, then govern the real-world enforcement timeline via staff letters, technical guidebooks, and portal readiness, turning compliance into a managed program rather than a single hard date.

  • 11. SEC Lets DTC Tie Net Debit Caps to Liquidity, Raising the Ceiling on U.S. Equity Settlement

    Date: 2026-02-03 (Federal Register publication; SEC order dated January 29, 2026)

    Regulator / Infrastructure: U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC); The Depository Trust Company (DTC) central securities depository and settlement system

    Theme: The SEC approves a DTC rule change that replaces fixed Net Debit Caps with caps that scale against DTC’s qualifying liquid resources, quietly rewriting how much intraday settlement risk the core U.S. securities plumbing can carry.

    • From hard cap to function of liquidity: DTC’s Net Debit Caps—historically set at $2.15B per participant and $2.85B per affiliated family—will no longer be static numbers. Under the approved rule change, caps will instead be set “based on DTC’s liquidity resources, related costs, and projected benefits to Participants,” and must always sit below DTC’s total qualifying liquid resources.
    • Qualifying liquid resources behind the cap: The order describes DTC’s current qualifying liquid resources as a $1.15B Participants Fund plus a $1.9B committed line of credit—about $3.05B in total—supplemented going forward by up to $3B of senior notes under an already approved debt-issuance programme. Net Debit Caps will now be tuned explicitly against this pool, rather than a fixed $2.15B / $2.85B ceiling.
    • Fewer emergency wires, smoother throughput: A confidential Impact Study, summarised in the order, indicates that increasing caps by roughly $0.75–$1B would have cut daily Settlement Progress Payments (SPPs) by about $3.62–$4.43B across 17 large participant families over the study period—evidence that higher, liquidity-linked caps can reduce recycling and last‑minute funding maneuvers.
    • Who funds the Liquidity Fund now shifts: The Liquidity Fund portion of the Participants Fund was previously paid only by affiliated families with caps above $2.15B. With caps now able to exceed that level for standalone firms, unaffiliated participants whose caps cross $2.15B will be pulled into Liquidity Fund contributions, redistributing the cost of shared settlement liquidity.
    • Affiliated family governance tightened: The Settlement Guide adds formal definitions for Affiliated Family and Unaffiliated Participant, clarifies how an aggregate family Net Debit Cap is shared among members, and amends Rule 9(B) so that no transaction may process if it would push an affiliated family over its aggregate cap—regardless of which entity is instructing the trade.

    Why this matters: Net Debit Caps are one of DTC’s main throttles on intraday settlement risk. Turning them into a parameterised function of qualifying liquid resources—backed by prefunded funds, credit lines, and new senior notes—effectively makes the maximum load of the U.S. equity settlement system a tunable policy choice rather than a fixed number. At the same time, pulling very large standalone firms into the Liquidity Fund and formalising “affiliated family” definitions reshapes who pays for, and who is constrained by, that shared liquidity pool. Market participants should treat this as a structural change in the control plane of U.S. securities clearance and settlement, not a housekeeping edit.

    Sources: Federal Register Document 2026-02117, Self-Regulatory Organizations; The Depository Trust Company; Order Approving Proposed Rule Change To Modify the DTC Settlement Service Guide and DTC Rules as They Relate to the DTC Net Debit Cap (91 FR ~4997, Feb. 3, 2026).     • NHTSA frames the findings as reaffirming the importance of the new female THOR dummy and of accounting for biological and biomechanical differences between women and men when designing and evaluating vehicle safety systems.

    • Eliminates mixed helicopter and fixed-wing traffic in the most congested part of the DCA approach and departure environment by restructuring procedures so the two types of operations are separated near the airport.
    • Closes Route 4 between Hains Point and the Wilson Bridge to routine helicopter traffic to reduce conflicts with fixed-wing arrivals and departures to and from DCA.

    • Requires military aircraft operating in the affected area to broadcast their position using ADS-B Out, with updated agreements between the FAA and the military to support that requirement.
    • Eliminates the use of visual separation within five nautical miles of DCA, tightening separation standards in the terminal area.
    • Publishes revised helicopter routes and zones for the DCA region and nearby airports so that helicopter flows are moved farther away from DCA arrival and departure paths, alongside additional staffing and oversight at DCA.

    • The "Legacy Portfolio": The affected book includes a small but important subset of Legacy Swaps – pre-compliance-date uncleared swaps exempt from the CFTC margin rule (“Margin Legacy Swaps”) and pre-clearing-date interest-rate swaps exempt from the swap clearing requirement (“Clearing Legacy Swaps”).     • Structured to avoid novation/assignment: Staff Letter No. 26-03 emphasizes that the merger occurs by operation of law under state corporate law, not via novations, assignments, exchanges, transfers, or conveyances.
